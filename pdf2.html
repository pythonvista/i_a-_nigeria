Logline

A man wakes up in an ancient council chamber, bound by chains and accused of theft by the chiefs of Nigeria's tribes. But as the trial unfolds, he reveals himself to be not a thief, but the living embodiment of the nation itself—and he has come to pass judgment on them.

STORY

Synopsis

The Awakening

The film opens in a surreal, mist-filled forest where a man in a torn designer suit is hunted by a mob. He is beaten, stripped of his wealth, and left for dead. He wakes up in a timeless Council Chamber, chained before the Oba and chiefs representing every major Nigerian ethnic group.

The Accusation

The chiefs accuse him of stealing their resources—gold, oil, land. They see him as a corrupt politician or a foreign exploiter. They demand he return what he has taken.

The Revelation

The man laughs. He begins to recount their own histories—secrets only the land itself would know. He speaks of the Benin Bronzes, the Civil War, the oil spills, the stolen daughters of Chibok. He reveals that he is not a thief. He is Nigeria.

The Judgment

As he speaks, he transforms. The Nigerian flag glows from within his skin. He breaks his chains, shattering the chamber and the chiefs' authority. He walks out into a new dawn, free at last.

Characters

The Protagonist / The Entity

Initially appears as a wealthy, corrupt figure. Reveals himself to be an ancient, supernatural entity. He carries the weight of centuries of trauma and the hope of millions. He is weary but powerful.

The Council

The Antagonists / The Elite

The Oba: Ancient authority, silent observer.
Chief Adaeze: Sharp, educated, ruthless.
Chief Okoro: Boastful, loud, fearful.
They represent the leadership that has failed the nation.

04

Visual Style

High-contrast cinematography meeting magical realism. A journey from grit to the divine.

Act 1: The Grit

Handheld, frenetic energy. Cold blue tones. Mud, blood, and sweat. Visceral reality.

Act 2: The Shadow

Static, composed shots. Warm candlelight against deep shadows (Chiaroscuro). Claustrophobic.

Act 3: The Divine

Wide, epic angles. Blinding golden light. Smooth, floating camera movement. Surrealism.

Director's Statement

"This is not just a film. It is a reclamation."

I am telling a story about the soul of Nigeria. For too long, our narrative has been controlled by others—by colonizers, by corrupt leaders, by the media.

"I AM NIGERIA" is an allegory for a nation that is waking up. It is about the pain of our history, but more importantly, it is about the inevitability of our freedom.

We are using state-of-the-art technology to tell an ancient story. This film will screen at Sundance, Cannes, and TIFF because it speaks a universal language: the language of identity.

— Seun Fakuade
